01 — FoundationsDESIGN.md ↗

Principles that keep everything recognisably Leaf.

Six rules sit above every token. When a decision isn't covered by a specific spec, these decide it.

01

Lead with warmth and clarity

Warm neutrals, generous space, plain language. Nothing cold, nothing loud for its own sake.

02

Coral is a signal, not a coat of paint

One decisive coral moment per view. It marks what matters — never body text, never a full background of text.

03

Stone is the room, Ink is the voice

Stone tones set a calm, recognisable environment. Ink carries text and contrast. Canvas is for clarity.

04

Secondary colours are support actors

Use them for variety, categories, and campaigns — never to replace Coral as the brand signal.

05

Sentence case, medium weights

Sentence case over all-caps. Confident medium-to-semibold headings, balanced by lighter labels.

06

Prefer tokens over one-off values

Reach for a named token before a raw hex. Stable names keep product, marketing, and agents in sync.

Space, form, elevation & motion

The primitives every screen is built from. Each one has a stable token name — reach for the token, not the raw value. Full set lives in the :root block in DESIGN.md.

04 — VoiceDESIGN.md ↗

Commercially sharp, plain-spoken, evidence-led.

Leaf turns unreliable marketing signals into decisions operators can trust — so the writing leads with the mechanism, names the commercial cost, and lands on a practical consequence. Technically credible and willing to make the uncomfortable point, without blaming the buyer. Never hypey, never vague.

We write

  • "Server-side tracking that closes the gaps quietly costing you spend."
  • "Decision-grade reporting on shared metric definitions — not another dashboard."
  • "Know what your marketing actually earned, in board-ready numbers."

We avoid

  • "Unlock growth with our seamless solution."
  • "Your single source of truth."
  • "Real ROAS, guaranteed."

07 — Imageryassets/imagery ↗

Iconographic, not photographic.

Leaf doesn't lean on stock photography. Our imagery is a single black isometric line icon, centred on a flat field of one secondary colour. It's calm, ownable, and made entirely from parts of the system we already have — the icon library and the palette.

Real Leaf Studio banners. One icon, one colour, generous breathing room — the topic drives both the icon and the field colour (people → Heather, decisions → Harbor, momentum → Marigold).

One icon, centred

A single black line icon from the library, optically centred, sized to roughly a quarter of the shorter edge. Never a cluster, never full-bleed.

Flat secondary field

The background is one solid secondary colour — never Coral, never a gradient, never a photo. Coral stays the brand signal, not a backdrop.

Black line only

Icons stay black line-art on the colour — no fills, no drop shadows, no recolouring to match. Contrast does the work.

Do

  • ✓Match field colour to topic, and rotate colours across a set for rhythm.
  • ✓Keep wide margins so the icon reads as one calm mark.
  • ✓Reuse library icons so imagery and UI stay one family.

Don't

  • ✕Use stock photography, 3D renders, or gradient meshes.
  • ✕Put icons on a Coral field or recolour them to the field.
  • ✕Crowd the field with multiple icons, text, or drop shadows.

08 — Photographyassets ↗

Let the brand be the hero.

Leaf usually stays out of the frame — our own imagery is iconographic (see Imagery). Photography is the deliberate exception. We reach for it to show customers in flight: real brands, real products, real moments. Leaf takes a step back and gains through the association — a light co-brand lockup, never a takeover.

Real co-brand posts. The customer's world fills the frame; Leaf appears only as a light lockup. We borrow the spotlight rather than compete for it.

The co-brand lockup

Whenever we share a customer's frame, the two marks lock up as Partner × leafsignal — separated by a lowercase ×.

Equal billing, light touch

Partner mark and leafsignal sit at the same visual weight, joined by a lowercase ×. Leaf never outsizes the customer it's celebrating.

White knockout on imagery

On photography the Leaf lockup is the Canvas (white) mark, set in a calm corner with clear space — no box, no heavy scrim beyond what the photo already gives.

Either brand can lead

Order flips by channel — partner-first on their story, Leaf-first on ours. The × stays the constant that ties the two together.

Customer quotes

For quotes and customer stories, the subject anchors the frame and their words lead — set on Ink, with Coral doing one job.

Their words lead

The quote is the largest element — Canvas type on an Ink surface, opened and closed by oversized Coral quotation marks. Coral appears once; nowhere else in the frame.

Subject to one side

Crop the person to one edge so the quote breathes. Attribution is name, role, and the partner logo — the customer is named, Leaf is not.

Campaign tag, quietly

A campaign line (e.g. "Elevating the Game") sits small in a corner in Canvas — a signature, never a headline.

Do

  • ✓Use photography for the brand-association moments — launches, partnerships, customer quotes.
  • ✓Let the partner's world fill the frame and keep the Leaf lockup light.
  • ✓Choose warm, real, in-context imagery that feels like the customer, not like Leaf.

Don't

  • ✕Reach for photography to decorate product UI or system imagery — that stays iconographic.
  • ✕Recolour or filter a partner's photography to Leaf's palette.
  • ✕Oversize the Leaf mark or let it crowd the co-brand lockup.

10 — For agents

Rules for generating on-brand Leaf artifacts.

Read this before producing a deck, page, post, or doc. When these conflict with a specific instruction, ask — don't guess.

Always

  • →Start on Light Stone. Use Canvas for dense or document-like content, Ink for high-impact moments.
  • →Ink for primary text, Warm Grey for secondary. One decisive Coral moment per view.
  • →Buttons come in two tiers. Primary: solid Coral with Canvas text. Secondary: transparent with a Coral border and Coral text. Coral is the brand colour — it carries the action, never a full content surface.
  • →For coral content blocks — cards, panels, slides — use soft Coral (a light tint) with Ink text, not a solid fill.
  • →Mona Sans for headings and body; Source Serif 4 (800 / italic 500) only for editorial or quote moments.
  • →Sentence case everywhere. Ink text on Coral, Aqua, and every secondary fill.
  • →Black SVG icons, consistent within a set, ~⅓ the width of a coloured tile.
  • →Reference tokens by name, not raw hex. Generous whitespace, ~14px radii.
  • →Set copy in Ink or Coral only — plus Warm Grey for secondary text on light surfaces, and Aqua for small labels on Ink/dark surfaces (like this one). The secondary and state palettes are for fills, dots, charts, and category fields, never for setting type — their tones aren't strong enough to hold contrast at text sizes. Build small-text hierarchy with weight, not faint colour. The one exception: a state tone may label its own status — a Fern “success” or Ember “error” heading — at title size and weight.
  • →Status uses the state palette, never Coral: alerts share one structure — soft tint + solid left accent + icon + Ink copy (Info Tidal, Success Fern, Warning Amber, Error Ember).
  • →Numbers: tabular figures, right-aligned; abbreviate currency at ≥1M; ratios 2 dp with ×, percentages 1 dp; deltas signed with ▲/▼ (Fern/Ember); dates as 1 Jun 2026; missing data is an em-dash, never blank or 0.
  • →Imagery is iconographic, not photographic: one black line icon centred on a flat secondary-colour field (never Coral, never a gradient or photo). No stock imagery.
  • →Meet WCAG AA: text ≥4.5:1, a visible 2px focus ring, 44px targets, and never colour as the only cue — pair it with a glyph, label, or icon. Honour prefers-reduced-motion.

Never

  • ×Coral, Aqua, or a Stone tone as body text. Canvas/white text on Coral for anything longer than a short button label.
  • ×A secondary colour dominating a full page or standing in for Coral as the signal.
  • ×Source Serif for UI, tables, forms, nav, or small labels.
  • ×ALL-CAPS labels, or Title Case on buttons and headings.
  • ×Hypey marketing language, invented hex values, or cropping padded logos.
  • ×Gradients, emoji, or decorative effects that aren't part of the system.
Primary action Secondary

Coral is the brand colour, used the way Stripe uses purple.

Primary: solid Coral, Canvas text, darkens on hover. Secondary: Coral outline and Coral text, soft-tint fill on hover. One primary per view.

Decks

Light Stone slides; one Coral or Ink section-break per deck. Serif only for the opening statement and quotes.

Web pages

Light Stone canvas, Canvas cards, Ink text. Coral for the single primary CTA. Sentence-case nav.

Social

Ink or Coral field, icon watermark, one line of serif. Secondary colour to distinguish a series.

Docs
